Prior Learning Assessment (PLA)

Improving student outcomes by honoring learning from outside the classroom.

by kelsey anderson

Research on PLA has shown promising results for college outcomes

With ongoing public discourse around lack of affordability in postsecondary education and high unemployment rates resulting from the COVID-19 pandemic, innovations that reach adult learners and reduce college costs are timely. One such academic practice, Prior Learning Assessment (PLA), is already in widespread use and could have significant positive impacts.

PLA is when an institution of higher education recognizes learning that occured outside of the traditional college classroom and awards credit for it. This could be learning a student gained from work, serving in the military, taking online classes or any number of nontraditional sources.

Relatively little data about PLA exists, but a few studies have shown promising outcomes for students. This site applies findings from a 2019 study of PLA to nationwide student demographics to model what the difference between earning PLA credits or not could look like for someone's chances of earning a Bachelor's degree, how long it might take and how much it could cost.

This diagram represents all the students at the over 4,000 federally recognized degree granting colleges in the US.

The proportion enrolled in each type of college is shown at the left.

 

Some students attend more than one school before they graduate.

 

And some never leave their first college.

 

Finally, less than half of the students that begin college in the US end up with a Bachelor's Degree.

 

The proportion of students that earn a Bachelor's Degree varies a lot depending on the type of school.

The highest graduation rates are at private nonprofit universities.

The lowest are at public two year colleges, where students usually must transfer before they can complete a four year degree.

 

The exciting part about PLA credit is that research suggests it can dramatically raise these rates.

A large 2019 study says it raises graduation rates by an average of 17 points for individual students.

Applied across all demographics, if everyone could be granted PLA, national graduation rates could be much higher.

PLA also reduces the average time and cost of a degree because PLA credits are free or very inexpensive compared to tuition.

 

Research suggests PLA is particularly impactful for some groups with low graduation rates.

Like older students.

 

Low income students.

 

Latinx / Hispanic students.

 

And students at two year colleges.

 

As of 2018, 48% of US schools grant PLA.

PLA policy is largely driven by individual colleges though.

For-profit and two-year colleges are the most likely and four-year public and private universities are the least likely to allow it.

This poses a big problem for transfer students, who may lose the advantage of their PLA credits if the receiving school won’t recognize them.

 

PLA is worth collecting better data about, researching and expanding further.

Especially if statewide articulation agreements can be put in place to protect student academic progress when they transfer.
